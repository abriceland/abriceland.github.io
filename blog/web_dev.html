Web Development Revisited

I started out CSS week in February with a spirit of confidence and ended it reduced to a puddle of nerves. Positioning was my great downfall. Despite hours of trying, I was unable to position the main portion of my blog index page correctly. It kept showing up below the element on the left, a navigation bar. And because my blog pages use the same basic formatting as my blog index, all of those pages were affected as well

I am a high achiever. I am unused to failure in a learning environment. The inability to achieve a seemingly simple task was a huge blow to my ego. I ended up having to walk away with a broken website and a fear of CSS.

That fear kept me from doing as much as I wanted to my site. Had I not been running to keep up with the other work for Dev Bootcamp, I would say I avoided working on my website. But the truth is that I focused on Ruby and learning the logic of the programming languages and tried to push the embarassment my site represented for me to the back of my mind.

After poking around a bit the last several weeks, this week was the first one that I really tried to address my site again. Still, in order to be able to cope, I had to have my hand held. A bootcamp colleague took a look at my HTML and CSS code, tried a few things and was likewise mystified as to why this area kept getting pushed down below. Then when comparing the working index page with the broken blog index page, she noticed a discrepancy - my index page used a "section" tag, whereas my blog index page used a "main" tag.

She changed the "main" tag to a "section" tag and, lo and behold, that part was tranported into place next to the nav bar, as I had planned 6 weeks before! My CSS positioning wasn't the problem in the least! In an effort to use HTML5 tags, I chose "main", since I believed it to be descriptive of my section. What I didn't realize and hadn't found out onis that it either takes up the page width or must be flush left.

My friend's discovery and other helpful advice gave me the heart I needed to put more work into my site and improve its appearance and functionality and I am exceedingly grateful. Now, even though my site is far from perfect, it is dramatically better than it was. Even more importantly, I have gained the requisite courage to work with HTML and CSS again without fear and trepidation, which is critical for the intense weeks ahead in Dev Bootcamp.
